# Organize — Put It Where It Belongs

After clarifying what something is and what the next action looks like, you need to put it in the right place so it surfaces at the right time.

GTD uses several lists to organize your commitments. Each one serves a different purpose.

## Projects

In GTD, a **project** is any desired outcome that requires more than one action step. That's it. "Plan birthday party" is a project. "Reorganize the garage" is a project. "Apply for a new passport" is a project.

This definition is broader than most people expect. You probably have 30-100 active projects right now — and recognizing that is liberating, because each one only needs one clear next action to keep it moving.

### Projects in Tandem

Tandem supports three project types:

- **Sequential** — Tasks are done in order. Only the first incomplete task is the "next action." When you complete it, the next one automatically becomes available. This is the most common type.
- **Parallel** — All tasks are available at once. Use this when tasks can be done in any order.
- **Single Actions** — A container for related but independent one-off tasks that don't have a shared outcome.

Tandem's **cascade engine** handles the bookkeeping automatically. Complete a task in a sequential project, and the next task is promoted to your "next action" list without you lifting a finger.

You can also nest **sub-projects** under a parent project for complex outcomes with distinct phases.

For a deeper dive, see [[projects-and-next-actions|Projects and Next Actions]].

## Contexts

A **context** is the tool, location, or situation you need to do a task. The idea: when you're at your computer, show me everything I can do at my computer. When I'm running errands, show me the errands list.

Common contexts:

- **@Computer** — anything requiring your laptop/desktop
- **@Phone** — calls and messages
- **@Home** — household tasks
- **@Errands** — things to do when you're out
- **@Office** — work-specific tasks
- **@Anywhere** — tasks with no location requirement

### Contexts in Tandem

Set up your contexts in the **Contexts** page. Tandem auto-seeds common GTD defaults on your first visit, and you can customize, reorder, and color-code them.

Every task can be assigned a context, and the **Do Now** page lets you filter by context to see only what's relevant to where you are right now.

## Energy Level and Time Estimates

David Allen recommends choosing your next action based on four criteria: context, time available, energy available, and priority.

Most apps ignore energy and time. Tandem doesn't.

- **Energy level** (Low / Medium / High) — How much mental or physical effort does this take? After a long day, filter for "low energy" tasks and knock out the easy wins.
- **Time estimate** — How long will this take? If you have 15 minutes before a meeting, filter for quick tasks.

These filters combine with context on the **Do Now** page, so you can ask: "Show me low-energy tasks I can do @Home in under 15 minutes."

## Waiting For

When you delegate something or are waiting on someone else, create a **Waiting For** entry. Record who you're waiting on, what for, and when you expect it.

Review your Waiting For list during your [[reflect|Weekly Review]] to follow up on anything that's gone quiet.

## Someday/Maybe

Not everything that crosses your mind is a commitment. The **Someday/Maybe** list is for things you might want to do but aren't ready to commit to:

- "Learn Italian"
- "Build a standing desk"
- "Start a podcast"
- "Take a pottery class"

These aren't active projects — they're possibilities. Review them during your Weekly Review. Some will eventually become projects. Many won't, and that's fine.

## Reference Material

Some things aren't actionable at all — they're just information you want to keep. Tandem's **Wiki** is your reference system. Store meeting notes, how-to guides, contact details, recipes, anything you might need to look up later.

The wiki supports markdown formatting and wikilinks for connecting related articles.
